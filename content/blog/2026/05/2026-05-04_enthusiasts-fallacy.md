There's a phenomenon at the intersection of [self-selection bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-selection_bias) and [XKCD 2501](https://xkcd.com/2501/) I've noticed in my own thoughts about my hobbies. Let's call it "enthusiast's bias."

If you are interested in improving at some activity `foo`, you're likely to also follow foo-related blogs and newsletters, watch video guides on foo tips and tricks, periodically lurk on /r/foo, etc etc. Most of your information regarding foo will, naturally, come from people who are similarly enthusiastic, and therefore more practiced at foo than average. In this situation you're probably in the 95th percentile or higher for foo among the general population, just in virtue of consciously putting in any time and effort at all to improve, but because the people you're comparing yourself to are also in the upper percentile you don't *feel* like a relative expert in foo, you feel like a hack fraud amateur.

 As [previously discussed](/2026/04/running) I consider myself at best a middling runner. My normal speed is somewhere around 10-11 minutes per mile, slow enough that I am regularly pacemogged by people twice my age. My one official race time is around 2:10 for a half marathon, a ways down the right slope of the bell curve for my age group. Sometimes when reading running forums I get a bit discouraged by, say, a post from someone lamenting their "terrible" 2 hour half time, but I try to keep in mind that for most people running 13 miles continuously at *any* pace seems like an unreachable feat of superhuman athleticism. Recently I was walking around the city with some friends and happened to pass by the specific telephone pole where I turn around when doing a 10 mile run. I pointed this out casually and was met with incredulity that I could run from my apartment to this point and back on a regular basis. I found this pretty funny. I cannot stress enough how unimpressive it is among runners to do 10 miles at an 11 minute pace, but for non-runners (i.e. most people) it sounds insane.

Knowing about this bias is useful to avoid beating yourself up too much if you're not a world-class champion at whatever it is you do, plus it's also handy when on the outside of some activity looking in. I feel the same way about swimming that I imagine non-runners feel about running; I can paddle around in the pool at the Marriot just fine, but the idea of swimming *multiple miles* is bonkers to me. Yet people do regularly swim multiple miles like it ain't no thing, including of all people my own mother (who, to come full circle, has expressed disproportionate astonishment at how far I run in turn). Yet I can appreciate, intellectually if not intuitively, that lap swimmers are probably *not* all Ubermenchen made of stronger stuff than I, and that if I dedicated myself as much to swimming as I do to my existing hobbies I could probably reach the resounding heights of the 90th percentile as well. 

(A potential trap here - some activities are *actually* really really hard, such that most people will never be able to become even mediocre at them with any amount of practice. But I think most normal things you or I might do for fun are outside this category) 

The lesson to take away here is that there's often a step change between not trying at all and trying even a little bit, and additionally an *epistemic* step change in how you evaluate your own skill over that transition that can make you undervalue your own skill. To paraphrase the [Couch to 5k](https://c25k.com/) people, no matter how slow you're going you're still lapping everyone on the couch.
